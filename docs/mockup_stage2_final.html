FCV Assessment: Honduras Sustainable Connectivity Project
1
Contextual Awareness & Risk Analysis
The project correctly identifies Honduras' structural fragilities and recurring crisis cycles, with SUBSTANTIAL overall risk rating. However, the generic reference to “illicit activities” fails to name documented MS-13 and Barrio 18 territorial control in Cortés department — the project’s primary area of operations.
Sensitivity Anticipate
No explicit reference to RRA or systematic risk analysis despite HIGH-risk context. The project does not differentiate its approach based on Honduras’ criminal-violence classification — a standard development design applied to a non-standard context.
Sensitivity Differentiate
2
Targeting, Inclusion & Beneficiary Protection
Geographic targeting focuses on areas where 70–89% live in poverty — appropriate but doesn’t disaggregate by conflict exposure or displacement status. The blanket assertion of no Indigenous Peoples contradicts documented Lenca populations in western Honduras (Intibucá, Lempira, La Paz).
Sensitivity Differentiate
No conflict-sensitive protocols for managing a 1,500-worker labour influx through criminal-violence-affected corridors in Cortés. GBV Action Plan is planned but comprehensive security risk management — covering workers, communities, and supply chains — is absent.
Sensitivity + Responsiveness Anticipate
Despite noting 247,000 internally displaced Hondurans and connecting to a major Guatemala trade corridor, the project includes no displacement-sensitive targeting or regional spillover measures.
Sensitivity Anticipate
3
Operational Design & FCV Flexibilities
Implementation arrangements acknowledge SIT’s limited WB project management experience and plan capacity support — an appropriate adaptation for the institutional context.
Sensitivity Enhanced Toolkit
The project does not utilise any FCV operational flexibilities — no CERC, GEMS, TPM, HEIS, or alternative implementation modalities. In a criminal-violence context with active gang territorial control over transport routes, the absence of third-party monitoring and remote supervision tools represents a significant missed opportunity.
Responsiveness Enhanced Toolkit
4
Monitoring, Learning & Accountability
No FCV-specific indicators or conflict-sensitive monitoring protocols. Standard E&S monitoring is planned but lacks early-warning systems appropriate for a major transport project in gang-affected territory.
Sensitivity Anticipate
Limited connection between the project’s M&E system and citizen-state relationship building. The grievance mechanism is procedural — designed for complaints, not for strengthening accountability or institutional trust between communities and the state infrastructure agency.
Responsiveness Differentiate
5
Economic Resilience & Root-Cause Engagement
The project aims to facilitate travel to schools, health facilities, and labour markets but does not articulate how improved connectivity will address the economic drivers of gang recruitment, youth migration, or community-level economic fragility in Cortés and Santa Bárbara.
Responsiveness Jobs & Private Sector
No complementary linkages to IFC advisory, MSME support, or local economic development programming along the improved corridor — a missed opportunity to connect infrastructure investment to economic resilience.
Responsiveness Jobs & Private Sector
⚠
Do No Harm Check
3 addressed 4 partial 1 not addressed
The project partially addresses grievance mechanisms, GBV prevention, and geographic equity. However, security risks from labour influx in gang-controlled areas are not addressed — no protocols for worker safety, community protection, or supply chain security along transport corridors controlled by MS-13 and Barrio 18. Elite capture safeguards and inter-group tension monitoring are weakly addressed. The blanket Indigenous Peoples exclusion raises conflict-sensitivity concerns given documented Lenca presence.
FCV Sensitivity

The project demonstrates partial awareness of Honduras’ FCV context but falls short on specificity. Criminal violence dynamics are acknowledged generically but not named or operationalised. Indigenous rights analysis appears incomplete. Design adaptations exist (capacity support, GBV planning) but are not calibrated to the criminal-violence context type.

FCV Responsiveness

The project does not actively engage with root causes of fragility. Economic connectivity is framed as a development outcome rather than a pathway to address gang-driven fragility. No FCV operational flexibilities are leveraged despite HIGH-risk classification. The M&E system is procedural rather than designed to build institutional accountability.
